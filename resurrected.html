3 MINUTES INTRODUCTION

The Resurrected is a VR experience designed for the Oculus Rift. It was inspired by Michael Jackson's Thriller MTV. Interactors will resurrect from a coffin with other zombies, realize their identity, follow the invitation and dancing with Michael Jackson, and join a group zombie dance in the finale.

HOW TO CREATE SUCH INTERACTION EXPERIENCE IN VIRTUAL REALITY?

The interactor spawn in a coffin as a zombie. As he heards the Thriller song, he resurrect, push and open the coffin lid, then get out from the coffin.

Once resurrected, the interactor sees Jackson is leading a horde of zombies dancing on a stage while inviting the interactor as background dancer.

The interactor steps under the spotlight. As he imitates Jackson's move in the right way, the audiences under the stage will cheer and celebrate.

ANALYSIS

DESIGN TREND & PRINCIPLES

Combined with my previous Virtual Reality design experience, I did literature review of VR Experience Design, refered to several exisiting artifacts and summarized key design trend and principles that could be applied to our VR narrative.

Design Principle: Working with the Sense of Presence

There is no such thing as a neutral observer in VR. Presence in VR is the sensation of being in the space of a given experience, of sharing that space with characters, of being there. That sensation in turn leads to the need to be active, to have intentions, to play a role. Interactor will feel an increased sense of responsibility (Why am I here? and What should I do?) in VR environment. This is why games and genre stories that focus on intentions (e.g. escaping something; solving something) work particularly well.

As a new media platform, Virtual Reality changes the way of storytelling. Unlike movies which confined to telling a story within a frame that helped direct the user's focus, VR is not confined, but all around the user (Designing for VR | A Beginners Guide). Therefore, giving the user an opportunity to acclimate to the new experience, creating a buffer to allow the user to intentionally begin the experience is ciritical. Slow and progressive familiarization,visual clues, and tutorials should be provided.



Design Principle: Progressive Storytelling

Case Study - Shard VR Slide, London

Shard VR Slide is an adrenaline-fuelled 360-degree virtual ride that swoops and slides through the London skyline. Interactors are strapped into a moving chair, hurtle down an imaginary slide. The integration of a physical moving chair creates better immersion virtual reality experience.




Design Trend: Using Real Objects in Combination with VR to Boost Immersion

The experimentation of using real objects in combination with VR is on the rise, most recently with the emergence of HTC Vive trackers, which can be affixed to real world objects. It's not hard to imagine future VR team games, interactive presentations and even educational applications that use real world objects.

Case Study - Coco VR

• Self Awareness










Once game started, interactors spawn in a fitting room backstage facing a mirror. They will see other virtual characters in a similar setting, facing the mirror and trying on clothes.This effectively hints interactors "who am I, why am I here and what should I do."




When interactors spawn on the stage for performance, a character will give interactors a maraca, then every performer starts to shake. This clearly engages the interactor to join and shake with everyone else.

Case Study - Maliang and the Magic Brush

My previous VR narrative project has taught me the importance of scaffolding storytelling. Upon entering the VR experience, never expect people to know what to do and where to go. For that project, we let interactors spawn in a dark room and placed a candle in their view range, with an obvious flame symbol on top. Interactors will then instinctively approach the signal and create light to improve visibility. This part fucntions as tutorial which successfully taught interactors how to interact with the environment.
