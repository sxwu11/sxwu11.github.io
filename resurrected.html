The resurrected is a VR experience designed for Oculus Rift using the setting of Michael Jackson’s Thriller. We use diegetic methods to cue interactors to take actions and imitate the virtual characters. Also, we looked into ways of helping interactors identify their roles in virtual reality. Incentives used include companions to help identify roles, gestures, and diegetic interactions to motivate actions. (Chinese user, please click here to watch the video)

Explore narrative design in Virtual Reality that can help interactors to identify

themselves as part of the virtual world and engage with virtual characters.

ANALYSIS

DESIGN TREND & PRINCIPLES

Combined with my previous Virtual Reality design experience, I did literature review of VR Experience Design, see what are out there, analyse what they did well and summarize what we learn that could be applied to our VR narrative.

Working with the sense of presence

There is no such thing as a neutral observer in VR. Presence in VR is the sensation of being in the space of a given experience, of sharing that space with characters, of being there. That sensation in turn leads to the need to be active, to have intentions, to play a role. Interactor will feel an increased sense of responsibility (Why am I here? and What should I do?) in VR environment. This is why games and genre stories that focus on intentions (e.g. escaping something; solving something) work particularly well.

As a new media platform, Virtual Reality changes the way of storytelling. Unlike movies which confined to telling a story within a frame that helped direct the user's focus, VR is not confined, but all around the user (Designing for VR | A Beginners Guide). Therefore, giving the user an opportunity to acclimate to the new experience, creating a buffer to allow the user to intentionally begin the experience is ciritical. Slow and progressive familiarization,visual clues, and tutorials should be provided.



Progressive storytelling

Case study - Shard VR Slide, London

Shard VR Slide is an adrenaline-fuelled 360-degree virtual ride that swoops and slides through the London skyline. Interactors are strapped into a moving chair, hurtle down an imaginary slide. The integration of a physical moving chair creates better immersion virtual reality experience.




Design implications - An elevated platform to simulate a coffin

Based on our narrative, we want our interactors to resurrect from a coffin, which means they will get up from some sort of elevated platform. Therefore, I proposed using something physical to mimic the coffin to simulate the resurrect experience. This will help with immersion and self-awareness for interactors.


Using real objects in combination with VR

The experimentation of using real objects in combination with VR is on the rise, most recently with the emergence of HTC Vive trackers, which can be affixed to real world objects. It's not hard to imagine future VR team games, interactive presentations and even educational applications that use real world objects.

Design implications - Hint interactor with non-player character (NPC)

Inspired by Coco VR, adding NPC dancers in the narrative will effectively help with interactor's self-awareness and cue interactors to take actions.

Case study - Maliang and the Magic Brush

My previous VR narrative project has taught me the importance of scaffolding storytelling. Upon entering the VR experience, never expect people to know what to do and where to go. For that project, we let interactors spawn in a dark room and placed a candle in their view range, with an obvious flame symbol on top. Interactors will then instinctively approach the signal and create light to improve visibility. This part fucntions as tutorial which successfully taught interactors how to interact with the environment.



Design implications - Progressive dacing tutorial

We try to encourage interactors to imitate the dancing moves of Thriller, to make it easier for interactor, we should let a virtual charactor to give a step-by-step tutorial that breaks complicated moves into small and easy pieces. This will give interactors a chance to slowly and progressively familiarize what they should do in the virtual space.

DESIGN CHALLENGES

Our team decided to use Oculus with 3-camera sensors as our hardware platform. With all the design principles and design limitations in mind, we resolved design challenges and kept testing them to find better solutions for each critical moment.

Since we will encourage our interactors to dance in VR, maximize playable area is a top priority for better experience. Considering we were using sofa to simulate coffin, I proposed the design of a side-opened coffin to reserve more dancing space.

How to design & optimize a roomscale experience

A real sofa to simulate coffin

Maximize playable area

Inspired by our initial research, with the space and equipment available, I proposed to use a real sofa to simulate a coffin. Interactors will start by lying on the sofa, then getting up and continuing their VR experience.

It is my understanding that great VR experience design is not just about desgining a fascinating virtual world people see with a headset, but designing a physical space that echoed the virtual space which could further enhanced immersive experience. As an architect with deep understanding of how people interact with spaces, I tried to come up with roomscale design solutions that could optimize interactor's experience both physically and virtually.

How to design a scene that works with sense of presence

Scene design plays a critical role in telling interactor. Based on my previous VR design experience, it is the nature for any interactor to observe and understand where they are once entering a new virtual world. Therefore, our team proposed two major scenes that interactor will see after resurrected from the coffin. They seems similar but interestingly, convey completely different identity information.

Option-1: Stage Performance

PROS

  • Stage performance creates dramatic agency

  • Scene could be really expressive and engaging

  • Great fit for the narrative


CONS

  • Interactor might be confused who to follow


Option-2: Campfire Party

PROS

  • All participants are equally important

  • Interactor acts as part of dancing crew

  • Interactor clealy knows who to follow


CONS

  • Michael Jackson will not be at a campfire party

  • Less expressive and dramatic than stage scene

  • Less crew reduces motivation



VR is appealing mainly because it provides the opportunity for people to experience something dramatic and superrealistic in a virtual environment. Therefore, when designing a VR narrative, we should design a dramatic experience. As a result, our team decided to design a stage performance scene since it is more expressive, dramatic and engaging than a campfire party.

• WHAT WENT WRONG

We break the iconic move of thriller dance into two takes: raising left arm with left foot, then raising right arm with right foot. However, some of our testers could not trigger Michael's recognition because they do not raise their arm high enough into the trigger colliders.

Full-body avatar

We did not implement full-body avatar with Oculus, which means interactors could only see their hands while dancing. Surprisingly, none of our testers mentioned or complained about not being able to seeing their feet during the testing session. We did not find strong reasons to explain why this happened. However, we still believe that in future iteration, being able to see full-body avatar would maintain the immersion experience, especially when interactors are focusing on their lower body (e.g. when they are lying in a coffin).

Although foot tracking or even full-body tracking is the trend of VR, Oculus did not support foot tracking as of the time when we finishing our project. However, we found that although our testers knew we were not tracking their feet with Oculus, many of them still lift their feet unconsciously to follow the virtual character. We believe this was a great evidence that showed the success of our narrative. A powerful narrative could make the VR experience so immersive that goes beyond our expectation.

EXTERNAL TESTING

Dealing with rotation

As we were brining free dance session with more complex dancing takes, we discovered that rotation takes cause problems. There are moments when virtual characters turn around and dance with their back to audience. In this case, if our interactors imitate and also turn around, there will lose reference and do not know what to do. This problem could be resolved by adding another virtual dancer at the back of interactor.


Besides, most VR hardware nowadays does not support wireless experience. The goggle cord makes any rotation more than 180° really difficult. We hope that this problem could be solved with the mature of wireless VR hardware in the future.

The resurrection of NPC zombie dancers

Our team decided to use NPC zombie dancers to help interactors identify their role. Rather than letting zombie dancers dancing on stage at the beginning, We let them resurrect from the coffin to hint our interactors who they are and what to do next.

AFTERTHOUGHTS

The experience of entering a new world in virtual reality is like the experience of a newborn child observing the world.  We start to understand the real world by figuring out the meaning of our existence: where am I, who am I and what am I going to do. Then we learn how to interact with the world by imitation. This model could be comprehended exactly the same way as we are designing a VR experience.


The process of working with presence in Virtual Reality is actually the process of helping interactors to answer these philosophical questions. A VR Experience Designer should understand how to use narrative as the powerful design tool to convey the answers.

Working on this project reminds me of the title of a painting by French artist Paul Gauguin.

Where Do We Come From? What Are We? Where Are We Going?

Interestingly, one is asking himself/herself the similar questions while trying a VR experience for the first time.

“Where am I? Who am I? What am I going to do?”
