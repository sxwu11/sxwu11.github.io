Maliang and the Magic Brush was inspired by Google Tilt brush, a VR game that allows interactors to paint in an immersive 3D virtual space. Our team aims to take such experiences further by allowing interactors to instantiate and interact with the objects they paint in virtual space. We believe such experience could be achieved in VR, affording interactors the experience of dramatic agency. (Chinese user, please click here to watch the video)

INITIAL STORYBOARD INSPIRATION

“Magic Brush” is a Chinese traditional fairy tale. It is about a kind little boy Maliang who has a magic brush that can turn anything he draws into a real world artifact. We take cues from this ancient Chinese fairy tale. Interactors will be able to instantiate what they drawn in the 3D virtual space.

DRAW

Interaction inspired by Google Tilt Brush

Tile Brush allows user to paint in an immersive virtual 3D spaces. Users are presented with a virtual palette and brush, creates strokes that follow in the virtual environment. We would like to further improve such drawing/creating interaction to give our interactors dramatic agency.

INSTANTIATE

Narrative & dramatic agency inspired by "Magic Brush"

Harold is a curious little boy who, with his purple crayon, has the power to create a world of his own simply by drawing it. Inspired by the story, we want interactors not only being able to instantiate the objects they drawn, but also able to interact with the objects they drawn.

VISUAL CUE AS CONSTRAINT & MOTIVATOR

For our interaction prototype, our team decided to use visual cue (semi-transparent 3D symbol floating in the virtual space) to guide interactors. They will need to find the cue, make sure what they drawn overlapped with the cues, then trigger the instantiation of an in-game objects and push the story.


We will observe our testers' behavior, collect user feedbacks and conduct quantative/qualitative study on how to better design visual cues to constrain/motivate our interactors' behavior, which is the design challenge we identified for this project.

VERSION 0.2 - VIDEO WALKTHROUGH

SCENE DESIGN

祠堂 (Ci Tang) is a family temple or ancestral temple that can be found in rural ancient China. It is a building where the ancestors of a wealthy family are honored. A typical Ci Tang usually consists of three "进" (Jin, sheltered space), representing one family's three generations, and two "井" (Jing, atrium) connected by a long doorway.


Our narrative took place in ancient China. We also set a plot where a bad guy was running towards Maliang and trying to catch him. The linear and ceremonial layout of a Ci Tang made it a perfect architecture for our narrative. The long doorway will also give our interactor enough time to react when they saw the bad guy coming.

Affordance of unconventional interaction

Although we've described the background story and introduced the fairy Magic Brush to our testers before we started the test, many of our testers still fail in instantiating the wood patch.



Typical failures were caused by:

     - High expectations  (testers drew random objects and hoped to instantiate them)

     - Distance perceptions  (testers followed the outline but drew without approaching the visual cues)

     - Error tolerance (testers were not able to trigger the instantiation due to imperfect stroke overlapping)



Whenever we want to introduce an unconventional interaction to our interactors, do not expect them to know how to do it. Slow and progressive familiarization, visual clues, and guidance should all be used to help the user.

• Provide a tutorial as part of the narrative at the beginning

• Provide positive feedback to engage interactors

• Allow interactors to trigger instantiation with imperfectly overlapping strokes

VR is meant to be immersive and offer 360 degrees of intriguing visual content. As a result, it takes more efforts for interactors to comprehend visual information. Specifically, it is not easy to get them focusing on one thing in particular. This is why visual hierarchy, a design principle typically emphasized in 2D, is equally important in VR design.

• Reduce visual noises and distractions in 3D space, particularly in interactor's range of vision

• Design & trigger interactions in interactor's cone-of-focus

• Use sound cues and visual design to guide interactor's attention

• HOW COULD WE IMPROVE

Visual hierarchy in Virtual Reality

We bring the bad guy at 7 seconds right after game started. Most testers did not even realized the bad guy was coming since we fail in designing a way to attract their attentions. We arbitrarily triggered an event without visual guidance.

• WHAT WE LEARNT

Fitts Law in Virtual Reality

In VR Fitts law is still relevant. Designers should make the most import elements in the scene adequate in size, close to the central focus of the viewer, and comfortable in distance for gesture interaction. Interactive objects that are not adequate in size in VR could either hard to find or difficult to interact.

• Adjust the size of visual cues so that they could be easily identified and interacted

• Consider the convention of the symbol to avoid misinterpretation



• HOW COULD WE IMPROVE

• WHAT WENT WRONG

• WHAT WE LEARNT

We observed that some of our interactors have difficulties stretching arms to instaintiate the patch due to the huge size of the cross cue. They also have difficulties finding the second cue because it was too small to be discovered. Besides, we were surprised to see some testers perceived the cross cue as a stop sign, hence quickly step away from the door.

Refine trigger sketch of visual cue

As we were bringing in more objects in our narrative, we find our testers have difficulties instantiating objects with complex geometry or with multiple stokes. We need to refine the trigger sketch to provide better user experience.


Ladder


The ladder required 6 strokes to trigger. Most of our testers were not able to achieve it either because they could not complete the sketch in time (any existing stroke will disappear in 5 seconds) or because one/more of their strokes are offset due to arm stretch distance.

• Use fewer strokes for a trigger sketch to improve the chance of instantiation

• Use simple lines and geometries in trigger sketch to enhance drawing experience

DESIGN IMPLICATIONS

Wings


Although interactor could complete a wing with 1 stroke, the outline of it was so complex that most interactor use 4 strokes instead. This will result in awkward gesture and lower lower the chance of instantiation.

More ways of interacting with the environment

According to user testing data, our tutorial was very effective in teaching our testers the "Draw-Instantiate" concept. The main problems were caused by the new interaction we introduced in this iteration as the narratives were becoming more complex.

• Be careful to bring in new ways of interaction without tutorial

• Consider user's mental model when designing an action

• Implement simple gesture/motion when interacting with objects


DESIGN IMPLICATIONS

Climb the Ladder


We introduced an a "Drag & Pull" action for interactor to climb the ladder. However, most interactors do not understand they should hold the trigger and drag to climb.

Fly with Wings


We introduced a fly action. Interactor could fly by swinging their arms. This interaction is comparatively well-received since it is gesture-based.

AFTERTHOUGHTS

On-screen instruction, as a traditional way of guiding interactors' behavior, is a convention that has being well-established on 2D media platform. We believe virtual reality should have a corresponding but unique approach of achieving the same goal.


Virtual reality, as a new media platform, does not currently have conventions of interaction yet. In our experiment, we established an effective "draw-instantiate" convention, which constrains and motivates our interactors at the same time. However, the ultimate experience of VR interaction should be unlimited and unconstrained, just like the movie Ready Player One. With the mature of VR hardware, it is our hope that in the future there is no need to learn how to interact in the virtual world: people could interact, live, work and entertainall in the VR world just like in real world.

There is a famous saying by Martin Leblanc in UX design industry:

“A user interface is like a joke. If you have to explain it, it’s not that good”.

I believe this could be apply to VR interaction design as well:

“An interaction in VR is like a joke. If you have to explain it, it’s not that good”.?
