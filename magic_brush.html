3 MINUTES INTRODUCTION

Maliang and the Magic Brush is a VR experiment designed for the HTC Vive and other room-scale VR devices. It was inspired by Google Tilt brush, a VR game that allows interactors to paint in an immersive 3D virtual space.


Our team aims to take such experiences further allowing interactors to instantiate and interact with the objects they paint in virtual space. We believe such instantiated experience is a good fit for VR narrative, affording interactors the experience of dramatic agency.

INTERACTION INSPIRATION

“Magic Brush” is a Chinese traditional fairy tale. It is about a kind little boy Maliang who has a magic brush that can turn anything he draws into a real world artifact.


Our narrative will take cues from this ancient Chinese fairy tale. Interactors will be able to instantiate what they drawn in the 3D virtual space.

DRAW

Interaction inspired by Google Tilt Brush

Tile Brush allows user to paint in an immersive virtual 3D spaces. Users are presented with a virtual palette from which they can select from a variety of brush types and colors. Movement of the handheld controller in 3D space creates brush strokes that follow in the virtual environment.


In our design, we would like to further improve such drawing/creating interaction to give our interactors dramatic agency.

INSTANTIATE

Narrative & dramatic agency inspired by "Magic Brush"

Harold is a curious little boy who, with his purple crayon, has the power to create a world of his own simply by drawing it. He wants to go for a walk in the moonlight, but there is no moon, so he draws one. He has nowhere to walk, so he draws a path.


Inspired by the story, we want interactors not only being able to instantiate the objects they drawn, but also able to interact with the objects they drawn.

VISUAL CUE AS CONSTRAINT & MOTIVATOR

For our interaction prototype, our team decided to use visual cue (semi-transparent 3D symbol floating in the virtual space) to guide interactors. They will need to find the cue, make sure what they drawn overlapped with the cues, then trigger the instantiation of an in-game objects and push the story.


We will observe our testers' behavior, collect user feedbacks and conduct quantative/qualitative study on how to better design visual cues to constrain/motivate our interactors' behavior, which is the design challenge we identified for this project.

SCENE DESIGN

祠堂 (Ci Tang) is a family temple or ancestral temple that can be found in rural ancient China. It is a building where the ancestors of a wealthy family are honored. A typical Ci Tang usually consists of three "进" (Jin, sheltered space), representing one family's three generations, and two "井" (Jing, atrium) connected by a long doorway.


Our narrative took place in ancient China. We also set a plot where a bad guy was running towards Maliang and trying to catch him. The linear and ceremonial layout of a Ci Tang made it a perfect architecture for our narrative. The long doorway will also give our interactor enough time to react when they saw the bad guy coming.

TUTORIAL SHOULD BE PROVIDED AS PART OF THE NARRATIVE

Although we've described the background story and introduced the fairy Magic Brush to our testers before we started the test, many of them still fail in instantiating the wood patch.



OBSERVATION FROM USER TESTING SESSIONS

• 4 out of 8 testers found the cross cue confusing and did not know what they should do

• 6 out of 8 testers failed in instantiating the wooden patch at their first try

Typical failures were caused by:

     - High expectations  (testers drew random objects and hoped to instantiate them)

     - Distance perceptions  (testers followed the outline but drew without approaching the visual cues)

     - Error tolerance (testers were not able to trigger the instantiation due to imperfect stroke overlap)



In order to use the magic brush as a convention of interaction that constrains gestures and meet interactors' expectations, an in-game tutorial should be included at the beginning of the narrative. They should also be designed as part of the narrative to maintain immersivity.

• Provide a simple tutorial as part of the narrative at the beginning

• Allow interactors to trigger instantiation with imperfectly overlapping strokes

• Add particle effects to brush tip when it contacts the visual cue as a positive feedback

DESIGN IMPLICATIONS

Right after we started the game, testers' initial attention were not on how to play the game but on comprehending what world they were in. There typical actions included looking around the scene, checking the function of buttons on controllers and walking around.


Not having considered such fact, it was obviously that we arbitrarily pushed the narrative by bring the bad guy at 7 seconds right after game started (if our testers failed to instantiate the patch before the bad guy ran in, they would be caught and the game was over). Most testers indicated that they did not even realized the bad guy was coming from outdoors since they were focusing on understanding the environment.

• All testers spent some time on perceiving the virtual world once entering the game

• 7 out of 8 testers were caught by the bad guy at the first try

• 6 out of 8 testers did not think the NPC is a bad guy

• Give interactor enough time to explore the game space

• Trigger the bad guy break-in event by interactor's action

• Use sound design to help with the narrative

• Design interactor's mood in game so it forms a positive cycle

OBSERVATION FROM USER TESTING SESSIONS

VISUAL CUES SHOULD BE SUBTLE BUT ABLE TO ATTRACT ATTENTION

While designing the shape of the visual cue, our team wants to abstract them into simple symbol so that they could be easily instantiate without too many strokes. However, it was unexpected for us to see that some testers perceived the patch cue as a stop sign due to the cross shape and hence quickly stay away from the door. They also have difficulties finding the second cue because it was too subtle to be realized.

• 2 out of 8 testers misperceived the meaning of the cross cue

• 3 out of 8 testers needed guidance to identify the next visual cues

OBSERVATION FROM USER TESTING SESSIONS

• Carefully design and position the cues to avoid misinterpretation

• Exaggerate the shape of cues so that they could be easily identified

TAKEAWAY & NEXT STEP

TRIGGER SKETCH OF VISUAL CUE SHOULD BE SIMPLIFIED

As we were bringing in more game objects with complex geometry in our narrative, more usability problems occurs while our testers trying to instantiate them. Instantiating ladders and wings are still the most difficult actions for our testers.


OBSERVATION FROM USER TESTING SESSIONS

• 8 out of 10 testers failed in instantiating ladder at their first try

• 4 out of 10 testers failed in instantiating wings at their first try

Ladder


The ladder required 6 strokes to trigger. Most of our testers were not able to achieve it either because they could not complete the sketch in time (any existing stroke will disappear in 5 seconds) or because one/more of their strokes are way off due to over-stretched distance.

• Use fewer strokes for a visual cue to improve the success rate of instantiation

• Use simple lines and geometries in trigger sketch to enhance drawing experience

DESIGN IMPLICATIONS

Wings


Our prototype required interactors to sketch the outline of wings to trigger the instantiation. Although interactor could complete a wing with 1 stroke, the shape of it was so complex that most interactor use 4 strokes to complete it, which would lower the sketch experience and result in failure of instantiation.

THE "INTERACTION" CONSTRAINTS NEEDED TO BE ESTABLISHED

OBSERVATION FROM USER TESTING SESSIONS

According to user testing data, the tutorial phase was very effective in teaching our testers the "Draw-Instantiate" concept. The main problems were caused by the new interaction we introduced in this iteration as the narratives were becoming more complex, We need to further think about the "Interact" part to constrain our testers.

• 1 out of 10 testers needed guidance on how to instantiate the wooden patch

• 7 out of 10 testers needed guidance on how to climb the ladder

• 3 out of 10 testers needed guidance on how to fly

• Consider user's mental model for interacting with an object before bringing it to the virtual environment

• Implement simple motion as a way of interacting with instantiated objects (Flying is a good example)

• Should we automatize the interaction? (Like Scribblenauts?)

DESIGN IMPLICATIONS

Climb the Ladder


To keep immersivity, we hoped our interactors to climb the ladder using a "Drag & Pull" action while holding the trigger. However, interactors had difficulty in understanding "Drag & Pull", since they've already got controller in hand.

Fly with Wings


To keep immersivity, we hoped our interactors to fly by swinging their arms (like a bird flapping its wing). This interaction is comparatively well-received than the climb one. Possibly because flying by waving wings matched with people's mental model, and the motion was simpler than the ladder climb.

AFTERTHOUGHTS

On-screen instruction, as a traditional way of guiding/constraining our interactors' behavior, is a convention that has being well-established on 2D media platform. We believe virtual reality should have a corresponding but unique approach of achieving the same goal. In one sentence, the biggest challenge we have during our experiment is:


How to use the magic brush as a convention of interaction that constrains gestures, while motivating interactors with the unique power VR brings that is beyond ordinary experience.



Virtual reality as a new form of media carrier, do not currently have a stable convention for such interaction yet. In our experiment, we established an effective "draw-instantiate" convention by implementing 3D visual cue. Based on our test data, we believed we were able to constrain and motivate most of our interactors throughout the narrative with very few interpretation. Nevertheless, what we did not consider well enough is the "interact" part of our concept. While we were bringing more interactable objects in our narrative, we should have considered if a new convention should be introduced (which will add another layer of complexity to our design challenge), or if the established convention could be comprehended as a way of understanding the interaction of new objects.


In Virtual Reality environment, interactor themselves become part of the narrative. This means we as the designer cannot gain full control of the narrative. What we can do is to predict their behavior based on the mental model, and design accordingly.
