II. DRINK IDENTIFIER - "I WANT EFFICIENT AND ACCURATE SERVICE"

Based on our findings from the online survey, one of the primary customer pain points distills down to communication between them and the baristas. Between communicating orders and the transferring their name to be called by, customers have a hard time determining what the barista actually knows about their order. This also can be amplified by orders that involve passing along from the cashier to the barista that makes and delivers the order. The name miscommunication problem is so well known, that there is a certain irony with the restaurant with customer connection through individual names as a brand identity, is known for failing at this.

I. PEAK HOUR PURCHASE - "I WANT TO MAKE GOOD USE OF MY TIME"

Our task analysis and on-site observations indicated that sometimes consumers will need to wait for 20-30 minutes to get their drinks during peak hour, even if they place their orders in a short period of time. There is no way for them to know how long it takes barista to make their drinks. Due to the unpredictability of time needed, customers are hesitating if they should grab a coffee when seeing a long line during peak time.

III. COFFEE JARGONS - "I DON'T WANT TO ORDER FROM THE DEFAULT MENU ALL THE TIME"

Starbucks provides many coffee products, and has developed its own set of jargon. Based on our interview records and affinity mapping studies, for people who are unfamiliar with coffee products but still go to Starbucks occasionally, they have difficulties in understanding these jargons. For frequent users, many of them only know about the things they order frequently, so they also need to struggle with the complicated Starbucks menu system and customization options.  All these factors will greatly slow down the purchase progress, which is especially bad during peak hours.

BRAINSTORM SESSION & CONCEPTUAL SKETCHES

We recruited 5 participants to join our sketch feedback sessions and evaluate our three conceptual sketches. Their feedbacks indicated a small series of kiosks that integrated multiple concepts will be the most appropriate solution. Our group then planned to develop a low-fidelity prototype and prepare for a wireframe feedback session.


Once the wireframe prototype was done, our group got together and developed a set of questions for the feedback session.  We set up three different tasks for our users and will ask them to perform one or two tasks on our wireframe prototype. Our group split into pairs and conduct feedback sessions separately. During each feedback session, we have one team member as facilitator and another team member as moderator and note taker.


We asked 6 people to participate in our feedback sessions for the wireframe, during which we hoped to know whether the user flow matches users needs and how design features can be improved.


All feedbacks were documented in a consistent way following the planned questions. Overall, our participants thought the system was easy to use with predictable button options and rather clear instructions, some of them believed they will definitely use the kiosk to order to prevent awkward communication with baristas.

Once our team had identified our problem space and user needs, we proceeding into brainstorming concepts that could be possible solutions. Through a long  round of concept generation, we were able to categorize over 40 concepts into trends and specific problems they were solving. We noticed our solutions tended to follow three different types of interaction from the users: a standalone digital interface, audio communication, and representation of a name.

WIREFRAME & TESTING

Low fidelity wireframe

We were able to get a large amount of information from our final hybrid prototype that turned into a substantial amount of qualitative and quantitative feedback. However, due to time constraint of our project, we might have approached the testing phase a little too quick. This resulted in our evaluations running a little shaky, despite receiving reliable results. Plus, we focused on collecting qualitative data rather than quantitative data this time based on the method we chose. If we had chance to get a larger sample size of participants, we would be able to gain more quantitative data to support/doubt our design decisions. That being said, we were able to adapt fairly well and turn our evaluations into results fairly painlessly and learn what should happen in future iterations.


A further step we could take is to test our products in the authentic Starbucks environment during peak hours, so we can really know whether our design can help to relieve pressure in queues and attracts users who have social anxiety when talking to baristas.

Design goal: Improve Starbucks In-store Purchase Experience during Peak Hours

Loyal Starbucks members are more likely to use the app, while a majority of occasional visitors still make most of their coffee orders in physical stores.
