DEFINE PROBLEM SPACE

At the first phase of our research, we used observations, semi-structured interviews, survey questions and literature reviews to better understand different purchasing contexts and identify potential user pain points at Starbucks.


Based on our observations and interviews, most Starbucks customers mentioned about their purchase experiece at physical store. As a result, we choose to focus on physical store purchase experience at the register (either standard stores or reserves, whose purchasing experience are almost the same). We think there are lots of possibilities for in-store experience improvement.

BRAINSTORM SESSION & CONCEPTUAL SKETCHES

Once our team had identified our problem space and user needs, we proceeding into brainstorming concepts that could be possible solutions to address the 3 main pain points we discovered above. Through a long  round of concept generation, we were able to categorize over 40 concepts into trends and specific problems they were solving.

Brainstorm session

IDEATE

A SELF-SERVICE PUBLIC KIOSK

Self-Services Kiosk Alleviated "Line Anxiety"

Some customers can experience “line anxiety” when people are waiting behind them to order, so they may pass over upsell options to place their orders quicker, or because the menus on the wall are confusing and hard to read. With a well-designed kiosk diverting customer flow, line anxiety is alleviated. In addition, since customers are guided through tempting photos of items, kiosks have been shown to increase the average sale size.

According to a report from Software Advice, forty-seven percent of survey respondents say order customization is their favorite self-ordering feature. Based on our user interview, many Starbucks customers has complained about the experience of ordering a customizated coffee. Thus, having a kiosk would greatly improve the user experience in order customization and increases the likelihood for repeat visits.

Self-Services Kiosk Allows Better User Experience in Order Customization

Self-Services Kiosk Improves Service Accuracy

The mobile ordering system encourages users to utilise their phones as self-service ordering systems, yet instore is seeing baristas struggling to keep up with the increase of orders while making mistakes. By integrating self-service kiosks, Starbucks could move more staff onto creating the coffees, improving efficiency and consumer satisfaction.

After the brainstorming session, we all agreed that a public self-service kiosk that integrated multiple concepts will be the most appropriate solution. Our design decision was evidenced by our user interview and secondary research of self-services for casual Quick Service Restaurant (QSR) like Starbucks.

This is not an exhausted list of advantages that self-service kiosk could bring to Starbucks customers. Not only could we bring better user experience to customers, but in fact, everything from operations to sales to management could be much simpler.


Considering our aim is to improve Starbuck's in-store purchase experience during peak hours at Georgia Tech Campus, where most customers are more likely to "order and go", our group decided to design a public self-service kiosk for Starbucks. We then planned to develop a low-fidelity prototype and prepare for a wireframe feedback session.

Why a public kisok?

Once the wireframe prototype was done, our group got together and developed a set of questions for the feedback session.  We set up three different tasks for our users and will ask them to perform one or two tasks on our wireframe prototype. Our group split into pairs and conduct feedback sessions separately.


WIREFRAME & TESTING

Kiosk home screen that offers simple call-to-action section: order, scan and avatar generator.

Users who still prefer to purchase at the register are provided the choice to use an avatar. They could roll a random avatar as their temporary in-store ID. When an order is ready, avatar name instead of a real customer name will be called and displayed on a monitor at the counter, which will resolve the name call issue.

Users could scan barcode on in-store items at the kiosk. They could proceed to check out by tapping shopping cart. If they still want to order food, they could tap the back button and access Starbuck’s food menu.

The main ordering menu lists all Starbuck product categories on the side bar. Once user made his selection, he could tap the drink and set up size, quantity and customization.

Users can tap the shopping cart icon anytime when they are ready to check out. They could modify or delete their orders here. They could also roll a random avatar as their in-store ID. If they choose not to use an avatar, they could type their preferred name into the system.

PROTOTYPE

We asked 6 people to participate in our feedback sessions for the wireframe, during which we hoped to know whether the user flow matches their anticipation and how design features can be improved.


Overall, our participants thought the system was easy to use with predictable button options and rather clear instructions, some of them believed they will definitely use the kiosk to order to prevent awkward communication with baristas. However, we do receive some valuable feedback regards to information architecture design and functionality.

This was my first user experience design project in which I went through all necessary phases of User-Centered Design.


I participated in user interviews and conducted on-site observations, then brainstormed design ideas with the team. I set up basic personas, came up with user journey map and identified design chances.
